## Classification criteria you actually used
- **Assumption vs. Context**: "Assumption" was used for conditions that, if violated, would fundamentally break the model's expected behavior or the interpretation of the task itself (e.g., "most ARC tasks are unambiguous"). "Context" described the factual setup or environment (e.g., "ARC-1", "test tasks", "Transformer blocks") without implying a condition for validity.
- **Mechanism vs. What happened**: "Mechanism" explained *why* the model behaves a certain way, often describing internal processes like attention focusing on specific structures or layers performing different types of connections. "What happened" was captured by "pattern," describing the observed outcome or trend (e.g., "successfully solved," "fails to solve," "attention patterns vary").
- **Intervention (implicit vs. absent)**: Interventions were generally explicit actions performed by the authors or the model (e.g., "solved by VARC," "solutions proposed by VARC," "model attempts to interpret the rule"). If the text described an outcome without an explicit manipulation, but the context implied the model's application, the model's action was considered an implicit intervention. For instance, showing "solved by VARC" implies VARC was applied.
- **Eval_metric vs. Pattern**: "Eval_metric" captured the specific measurement instrument or protocol (e.g., "correct output," "Vote," "per-pixel softmax attention maps"). "Pattern" described the observed trend or shape of these measurements (e.g., "successfully solved," "fails to solve," "matches Ground Truth," "patterns vary"). Numeric values were strictly moved to the `note` field for patterns and eval_metrics.
- **Rule of thumb**: For figures, I systematically went through the caption first, then the visual elements (grids, labels, heatmap descriptions) to ensure comprehensive coverage, treating each distinct piece of information as a potential node. I also consolidated similar `eval_metric` and `pattern` descriptions across figures when they referred to the same underlying concept (e.g., "correct output" vs "Ground truth").

## What you deliberately did NOT extract
I deliberately did not extract any clauses containing hedging language (e.g., "may admit multiple plausible explanations") as "assumption" if they did not pass the certainty gate. For example, "some may admit multiple plausible explanations or rules" was extracted as a pattern describing the nature of some tasks, but not as an assumption about the model's validity, because the "may" implies it's not a definite precondition that would break the claim if false. I also avoided extracting general statements about the paper's purpose or high-level claims that were not directly tied to an experimental setup or result.
