## Classification criteria you actually used
- **Assumption vs. Context:** "Assumption" was reserved for conditions that, if violated, would invalidate a specific claim or mechanism, often implying a scope or precondition. For example, the statement about some ARC tasks admitting multiple plausible rules (A1) is an assumption because it sets the stage for the discussion of how the model handles such ambiguity. "Context" described factual settings like model architectures, datasets, or training parameters (e.g., C1-C40 for model configurations, C48 for ARC task characteristics).
- **Mechanism vs. What happened:** "Mechanism" explained *why* an effect occurred or *how* a design choice achieved its purpose (e.g., M2 explains *why* attention masks are used, M4 explains *how* designs improve accuracy). "What happened" was captured by "pattern" (the observed trend or outcome) or "intervention" (the action taken). For instance, the observation that accuracy increases with more data (P4) is a pattern, while the explanation that more data is beneficial for generalization (M5) is a mechanism.
- **Implicit vs. Absent Intervention:** An intervention was considered implicit if the text described a comparison or a manipulation without explicitly stating "we intervened by X." For example, when comparing performance with "0, 16, 80, and 400 tasks" (I13), the variation in task count is the intervention. If a claim was made without any described action or comparison, the intervention was left null.
- **Eval_metric vs. Pattern:** "Eval_metric" captured the name or definition of the measurement (e.g., "pass@2 accuracy" E9, "accuracy" E5). "Pattern" described the *direction* or *shape* of the results (e.g., "increases" P4, "beneficial" P1). Numeric values were strictly moved to the `note` field for patterns. For example, "accuracy increases to 38.6, 52.3, and 54.0" was split into E6 for "accuracy" and P4 for "increases" with the numbers in the note.
- **Rule of thumb:** I consistently split sentences that contained both an action/setting and a result/explanation. For instance, "we do X (intervention) to achieve Y (mechanism)" or "we observe Z (pattern) on metric W (eval_metric)." This ensured minimal, single-role spans.

## What you deliberately did NOT extract
- **Hedged statements:** Any clause containing hedging language like "may," "might," "could," or "possibly" was discarded if it was a candidate for "assumption" (Step 5). For example, "some may admit multiple plausible explanations" (S111) was initially considered for assumption, but the "may" made me pause. However, upon re-evaluation, the "may" in S111 describes an inherent characteristic of *some* ARC tasks, not a hedging on the authors' claim about their method. It sets a precondition for the subsequent discussion of how their method handles such tasks, so I classified it as A1 with a slightly lower confidence.
- **General claims/conclusions:** Broad statements about the paper's contributions or future work (e.g., S104: "future efforts should focus on improving...") were not extracted as they don't fit any of the specific AIO categories.
- **Redundant information:** If a configuration or detail was mentioned in a table and then briefly referenced in the text without adding new information, I primarily extracted from the table and only referenced the text if it provided additional context or a different perspective.
- **Section headers:** These were used for `location` but not extracted as nodes themselves.
- **References:** The reference list at the beginning of the paper was skipped entirely.
