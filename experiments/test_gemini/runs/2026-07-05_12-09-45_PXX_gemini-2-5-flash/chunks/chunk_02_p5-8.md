## Classification criteria you actually used

- **Assumption vs. Context**: "Assumption" was reserved for explicit preconditions or validity boundaries that, if violated, would invalidate a specific claim or mechanism. This often involved conditional statements or explicit statements about what is *not* assumed. "Context" described factual setup details (datasets, models, parameters) that simply characterize the experiment without asserting a conditional dependency for a claim's validity. For example, "As there are very few demo pairs" is context, but "under the assumption that all auxiliary tasks are translation and scale invariant" is an assumption.

- **Mechanism vs. What happened**: "Mechanism" explained *why* an observed effect occurred or *how* a component contributed to a result (e.g., "to improve accuracy," "as a result of better fitting," "reflecting the influence of the pattern's core"). It often involved causal language. "Pattern" described *what* happened quantitatively or qualitatively (e.g., "gain of X points," "degradation," "performs better," "exhibit similar semantics").

- **Implicit vs. Absent Intervention**: An intervention was considered implicit if the text described a specific action or method taken by the authors to achieve a result or test a hypothesis, even if not explicitly phrased as "we intervened by X." For example, "we adopt multi-view inference" was an intervention. If a claim was made without any mention of a specific action or comparison performed by the authors to test it, the intervention was considered genuinely absent (null).

- **Eval_metric vs. Pattern**: "Eval_metric" strictly defined the measurement instrument or protocol (e.g., "pass@2 accuracy," "layer-wise map is the softmax attention map averaged"). "Pattern" described the *result* of that measurement (e.g., "yields 27.7 improvement," "achieve decent accuracy," "exhibit strong attention"). When both appeared in one sentence, they were split into separate nodes.

- **Rule of thumb**: I consistently applied the "no hedging" rule for assumptions. Any clause containing "may," "might," "could," or similar uncertainty was discarded from being an assumption, even if it described a potential limitation. This helped maintain a high bar for the "assumption" category.

## What you deliberately did NOT extract

- **Hedged statements about future work or limitations**: Clauses like "(we note that given the rapid progress of LLMs, these models may have stronger results by the time our paper is public)" or "Future research may extend this direction..." were discarded. They contained hedging language ("may have," "may extend") and did not state definite preconditions or outcomes.
- **General background information or common practices**: Sentences describing widely known concepts or practices (e.g., "It was a common practice to consolidate the predictions from multiple views") were generally discarded unless they directly served as context for a specific intervention or mechanism in the paper.
- **Section titles and figure/table captions that merely introduced content**: For example, "Figure 6. Effect of test-time training" or "Test-time training (TTT)" were not extracted as they are organizational rather than informative clauses for the defined categories.
- **Conceptual descriptions without concrete actions or results**: A sentence like "The test-time training followed by inference can be viewed abstractly as a function F(Xinfer | Demo)→ Yinfer" was discarded as it's a conceptual framing, not an intervention, pattern, or mechanism.
