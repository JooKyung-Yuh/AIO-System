## Classification criteria you actually used
-   **Assumption vs. Context:** I distinguished "assumption" from "context" by asking if the statement described a precondition that, if violated, would invalidate the core claim or the task's solvability. For instance, an unclear task rule (A1) is an assumption about the clarity of the problem definition itself. Context, on the other hand, describes factual aspects of the experimental setup, dataset, or task characteristics without implying a failure condition if changed (e.g., "ARC-1" or "test tasks").
-   **Mechanism vs. What happened:** "Mechanism" was reserved for explanations of *why* something occurred or *how* the model functions internally (e.g., "Layers at different depths tend to focus on different structures" or "model attempts to interpret the rule"). A plain description of "what happened" (e.g., "successfully solved" or "unsolved") was classified as a "pattern."
-   **Implicit vs. Absent Intervention:** Interventions were considered implicit when the text described the model's action or application (e.g., "solved by VARC," "solutions proposed by VARC," or "VARC processes the input grid"). If the model was performing an action to achieve a result, it was an intervention. If the text merely described a state or characteristic without an active manipulation, intervention was considered absent (null).
-   **Eval_metric vs. Pattern:** "Eval_metric" captured the specific measurement method or criterion (e.g., "Vote," "correct output," "per-pixel softmax attention maps"). "Pattern" described the observed trend or outcome using that metric (e.g., "successfully solved," "unsolved," "attention maps show varying patterns"). Numeric values were explicitly moved to the `note` field for patterns.
-   **Rule of thumb:** For figures, I prioritized extracting general trends and setup descriptions from captions and overall visual elements, rather than listing every specific instance or numerical value, to capture the main message of the visualization.

## What you deliberately did NOT extract
I deliberately did not extract clauses that contained hedging language (e.g., "may not hold," "could possibly") when evaluating for "assumption." For example, if a sentence stated "results may not hold on Y," I would extract "we tested only on X" as context but discard the "may not hold on Y" part because it did not pass the certainty gate for assumptions. In this paper, S14 ("some may admit multiple plausible explanations or rules") was initially considered for assumption but then classified as context due to the "may admit" hedging, describing a characteristic rather than a definite precondition. I also avoided extracting specific numerical vote counts for each task in Figures 17 and 18 as separate patterns, instead opting for generalized patterns of success/failure and noting that votes were 'often higher' for correct solutions.
