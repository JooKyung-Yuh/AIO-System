## Classification criteria you actually used
- **Assumption vs. Context**: "Assumption" was reserved for explicit or implicit conditions that *must* hold for a claim or mechanism to be valid, or for a specific experimental outcome to be expected. These often took the form of "since X is true, we do Y" or "if X were false, Y would break." "Context" described factual, descriptive elements of the experimental setup (model, dataset, task, parameters) that define *what* was done, without implying a dependency for validity. For example, "few demo pairs" leading to data augmentation is an assumption, while "canvas size is 64x64" is context.
- **Mechanism vs. Description**: "Mechanism" explained *why* an intervention produced an effect, often using causal language like "enables," "explains why," or "as a result of." It provided the underlying causal theory. A plain description of "what happened" (e.g., "we applied X") was classified as an intervention or context, while the observed outcome was a pattern.
- **Implicit vs. Absent Intervention**: Interventions were considered implicit if the text clearly described a comparison or manipulation (e.g., "we compare X with Y" or "starting from a baseline"). If a claim was made without any described manipulation or comparison, the intervention was genuinely absent (null). For figures/tables, the comparison shown (e.g., different model variants in a table) was treated as an implicit intervention.
- **Eval_metric vs. Pattern**: "Eval_metric" captured the name, unit, or definition of the measurement instrument (e.g., "pass@2 accuracy," "Accuracy (%)"). "Pattern" described the *trend* or *shape* of the measurements (e.g., "improves," "degradation," "outperforms"). Numeric magnitudes were strictly moved to the `note` field for patterns.
- **Rule of thumb**: I prioritized splitting sentences into multiple nodes if they contained distinct roles. For instance, a sentence stating "Since X (assumption), we did Y (intervention), which led to Z (pattern)" would be split into three separate nodes. Hedged statements (e.g., "may cause," "could lead to") were consistently discarded for assumptions and mechanisms.

## What you deliberately did NOT extract
- **Hedged statements**: Any clause containing hedging language like "may cause," "might lead to," "could extend," or "we hypothesize" was deliberately excluded from being classified as an assumption or mechanism. For example, "We hypothesize that overtraining on the test tasks may cause the model to forget..." (S127) was discarded.
- **Meta-statements**: Sentences that described the paper's structure or referred to other parts of the paper (e.g., "We elaborate on the details in the next section," "See Fig. 7(e,f)") were not extracted as they do not convey experimental evidence or claims.
- **Recommendations/Future Work**: Statements about future research directions or recommendations (e.g., "suggesting that future research should focus on generalization" in S120, or the entire S177-S179 block) were not extracted, as they are not factual claims or mechanisms about the current work.
- **Redundant information**: Duplicate sentences or clauses that appeared verbatim in multiple locations (e.g., S29, S30, S31, S32, S33 were duplicates of earlier sentences) were only extracted once to avoid redundancy.
